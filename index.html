Glossary of Key Terms

Every term is defined on its own, then anchored with a short "in practice" note so you can see how it shows up on a real program — written generically so it applies at your next job too.

Program & Governance
End ClientConcept

The organization actually receiving the work — the company whose business the program serves — as distinct from any outside vendor supplying software or any consulting partner supplying expertise. Keeping this distinction clear is useful on any program: you'll usually have one end client, one or more vendors, and sometimes a consulting partner layered in between.

In practiceThis guide uses "end client" as a stand-in for whatever company is actually implementing the program, so the concepts transfer cleanly to a different employer.
Strategic ProgramGovernance

A body of work formally recognized by leadership as important enough to warrant dedicated budget, executive sponsorship, and regular visibility — as opposed to a routine project run entirely within one team. Strategic programs typically get a steering committee, milestone reporting up the chain, and cross-team coordination that ordinary projects don't.

In practiceExecutive visibility raises the bar on documentation, communication cadence, and how carefully vendor decisions get evaluated before work starts.
Vendor EvaluationGovernance

A structured process for comparing outside vendors against defined criteria — cost, capability fit, security posture, integration effort, references — before committing to one. A real evaluation produces a scored comparison and a documented recommendation, not just a preference.

In practiceA consulting partner is often brought in specifically to run this evaluation objectively before any building starts.
Vendors & Consulting Partners
Third-Party Vendor (Client Portal / Portfolio Reporting)Vendor

An outside software company whose packaged product you configure and integrate with, rather than building the same capability from scratch. In wealth management specifically, the "client portal" category is usually filled by a portfolio-reporting platform: something that aggregates holdings across custodians and asset types and produces the performance and allocation views clients see.

Well-known names in this category include Addepar (built for complex, multi-entity, alternative-asset portfolios and considered the benchmark for ultra-high-net-worth reporting), Orion Advisor Tech (a broader all-in-one platform that also bundles CRM and trading), SS&C Black Diamond (known for polished, branded client reporting and a large integrations network), and Envestnet | Tamarac (common in firms already inside the Envestnet ecosystem). Each trades off implementation complexity against depth of reporting — the more complex the underlying portfolios, the more setup and ongoing operational ownership the platform tends to need.

In practiceThe pipeline's whole job is producing clean, correctly-shaped files this kind of vendor can ingest to power the client-facing portfolio and performance views.
Consulting PartnerPartner

An outside firm engaged to supply expertise the internal team doesn't have in-house — commonly vendor evaluation, general consulting, and technology strategy. A consulting partner advises and often staffs the program day to day, but doesn't own the underlying product being implemented.

One example of this kind of firm is Incedo Inc., a digital transformation, data, and AI consulting company founded in 2012 and headquartered in New Jersey, with additional delivery centers internationally. Incedo works across financial services, telecom, life sciences, and product engineering, pairing engineering and data-science capability with domain expertise, and holds a formal technology partnership with Databricks specifically around building and modernizing data platforms — directly relevant to a program like this one.

In practiceConsulting partners are typically where the "domain SME" running day-to-day KT sessions comes from.
Domain SMERole

"SME" stands for Subject Matter Expert. A domain SME understands the business rules, terminology, and real-world workflow of an area deeply — here, wealth management — but isn't necessarily the person writing code. Their job is making sure what gets built actually matches how the business works, translating business needs into requirements a technical team can build against.

In practiceDomain, not developer, is the key distinction: this role owns correctness of the business logic, not the implementation of the pipeline itself.
Scrum MasterRole

The Agile team role responsible for running process — standups, sprint planning, removing blockers — rather than doing hands-on technical or business-analysis work. A good Scrum Master protects the team's focus and keeps progress visible to stakeholders.

In practiceOn an executive-visible program, this role is often the connective tissue between the working team and leadership reporting.
Client Experience
Client PortalProduct

A self-service web or app interface that lets end clients log in and see their own information directly, without calling an advisor. In wealth management, this centers on account holdings, transactions, and performance — increasingly with mobile access expected as standard.

In practiceThe portal is the visible tip of a much larger back-end pipeline; most of the actual program effort goes into what feeds it.
Portfolio & PerformanceConcept

"Portfolio" refers to the full set of holdings — stocks, funds, cash, alternatives — in an account. "Performance" refers to how that portfolio's value has changed over time: returns, gains and losses, usually benchmarked against a comparison index. These two views are the core of what a wealth-management client wants from a portal.

In practiceThese are the two things the portal is ultimately built to show — everything upstream exists to get this data correct and current.
Data & Systems
Data Platform (Lakehouse)Platform

A unified data platform — often called a "lakehouse" — that combines large-scale data storage with the compute needed to process, transform, and analyze that data in one place, instead of storage and processing living in separate, disconnected tools. Databricks is a leading example, built on the open-source Apache Spark project and Delta Lake storage format, and is commonly used for data engineering, streaming, and machine-learning workloads.

Databricks is frequently compared to Snowflake, another major cloud data platform. Snowflake began as a SQL-first, fully-managed data warehouse optimized for business intelligence and reporting — minimal setup, strong for analysts running dashboards. Databricks leans toward large-scale engineering and ML workloads and gives teams more direct control over infrastructure. Many organizations run both together: a lakehouse platform like Databricks for ingestion and transformation, and a warehouse like Snowflake for downstream analytics and reporting.

In practiceThis is where source-system data lands, gets checked for quality, and has business logic applied before files are built for the portal vendor.
Databricks Architecture Deep-Dive
Apache SparkEngine

An open-source distributed data-processing engine that splits large computations across many machines so they run in parallel, instead of on one machine at a time. Spark can process data in-memory, which makes it fast for the kind of large-scale transformations a wealth-management pipeline needs, and it supports multiple languages — Python, SQL, Scala, and R — often in the same notebook.

In practiceDatabricks was built by the original creators of Spark, and Spark is the compute engine actually running the transformation code underneath every Databricks job.
Delta LakeStorage Format

An open-source storage layer that adds database-like reliability to files sitting in cheap cloud storage. Specifically, it adds ACID transactions (so a write either fully succeeds or fully fails — never leaves data half-written), schema enforcement (rejecting data that doesn't match the expected structure), schema evolution (safely adding new fields over time), and "time travel" (the ability to query a table as it existed at an earlier point, which is invaluable for debugging a bad run).

In practiceDelta Lake is the default table format across a Databricks pipeline — every layer of the pipeline is typically stored as Delta tables rather than plain files.
Unity CatalogGovernance

Databricks' centralized governance layer for data and AI assets. It provides fine-grained access control (down to specific columns, not just whole tables), a searchable catalog of what data exists and where, lineage tracking (showing exactly which upstream tables and columns fed into a given result), and audit logging of who accessed what.

In practiceThis is what lets a program prove — to auditors or to itself — exactly where a number in the client portal actually came from.
Medallion Architecture (Bronze / Silver / Gold)Pattern

A data design pattern, sometimes called "multi-hop," that organizes a lakehouse into progressively refined layers. The Bronze layer holds raw, largely untouched data exactly as it arrived from source systems — append-only, kept for history and auditability. The Silver layer cleans, deduplicates, and standardizes that data into a validated, joined view. The Gold layer holds business-ready, aggregated tables shaped specifically for reporting and downstream consumption.

Keeping the raw Bronze copy matters because it lets a team rebuild Silver and Gold from scratch if a transformation turns out to be wrong — without needing to re-pull from the original source system.

In practiceThe files eventually delivered to the portal vendor are effectively a Gold-layer output — the end product of data that has already passed through Bronze and Silver.
Batch vs. StreamingConcept

Two different ways to move data through a pipeline. Batch processing handles data in scheduled, discrete chunks — for example, a job that runs once every night. Streaming (in Databricks, using Spark Structured Streaming) processes data continuously as it arrives, which matters when near-real-time freshness is required rather than a daily snapshot.

In practiceMost portfolio and performance reporting is well served by batch processing on a daily schedule; streaming is reserved for cases where staleness of even a few hours would be a real problem.
Cluster & NotebookCompute

A cluster is the actual set of compute resources — virtual machines — that runs the processing work; clusters can scale up or down with the workload and shut off automatically when idle to control cost. A notebook is the interactive, cell-by-cell coding environment engineers use to write and run that processing code, mixing Python, SQL, and markdown documentation in one place.

In practiceAd hoc exploration usually happens in a notebook run manually on a small cluster; the finished logic then gets scheduled to run automatically as part of the production pipeline.
Source SystemConcept

Any system where data originates — the "system of record" for a particular kind of information, before it's copied or transformed anywhere else. Common examples on a wealth-management program include account-opening tools and the CRM.

In practiceEvery downstream number should be traceable back to a specific source system — that traceability is what makes debugging a bad number possible.
CRM (Customer Relationship Management)System

Software for tracking clients, advisors, interactions, and account relationships — the operational record of who a firm serves and how. In wealth management specifically, firms typically choose between a large, highly customizable horizontal platform and a purpose-built advisor CRM.

Salesforce, through its Financial Services Cloud product, is the dominant horizontal option — powerful and extensively customizable, but more expensive and more complex to configure, and generally favored by larger enterprise firms. Purpose-built alternatives like Redtail (the longtime market-share leader on affordability and integrations) and Wealthbox (newer, known for a modern interface and faster onboarding) trade some of that flexibility for lower cost and CRM features built specifically around advisor workflows, and are more common at small-to-mid-sized firms.

In practiceWhichever CRM is in use, it's typically one of the named source systems feeding client and account data into the data platform.
Data EcosystemConcept

The full collection of systems, pipelines, storage, and tools that together produce, move, transform, and consume an organization's data — the bigger picture that any one source system or pipeline is just a piece of.

In practiceEarly program work often includes mapping this whole ecosystem before committing to a specific pipeline design, so nothing gets missed.
Data Quality ChecksProcess

Automated validation rules that confirm data is accurate, complete, and consistent before it's trusted or passed downstream — checking for things like missing fields, out-of-range values, or mismatched records between systems.

In practiceThese checks typically run inside the data platform, before any file is generated for the vendor — catching problems before a client ever sees them.
Business LogicProcess

The set of rules that encode how the business actually wants data handled or transformed, beyond simply copying it from one place to another. Business logic is what turns raw data into the correct answer for a specific real-world use case — for example, how to treat an account differently depending on which custodian holds it.

In practiceBusiness logic is usually scoped around a specific, limited set of integrations rather than built generically for every possible case up front.
Exception QueueProcess

A holding area where records that fail a validation or business-logic rule get routed, instead of either silently passing through wrong or breaking the whole pipeline. Someone, or some downstream process, works the queue to fix or discard those records.

In practiceA healthy exception queue is actively worked, not just accumulated — an ever-growing queue is usually a sign the upstream data or rules need attention.
Production PipelineProcess

An automated data workflow running in the live ("production") environment on a real schedule, with monitoring and alerting — as opposed to a one-off script or something only running in development or testing.

In practiceThis is the machinery that reliably moves data from source systems to the vendor on an ongoing basis, not just for the initial rollout.
Custody & Integrations
CustodianConcept

A financial institution that physically holds and safeguards a client's securities and cash on behalf of the advisor or firm, and handles trade settlement and recordkeeping. Advisors direct the investment decisions; the custodian holds the assets and is subject to regulatory oversight designed to protect them.

The custody market is concentrated among a handful of large players. Charles Schwab is generally the largest, followed by Fidelity, then BNY Pershing and LPL Financial — together these four control the large majority of custodied assets among independent advisory firms in the U.S. Pershing tends to serve larger, more operationally complex firms and family offices; LPL operates as both a broker-dealer and a custodian, appealing to hybrid advisors; and newer, tech-first entrants like Altruist target smaller firms with modern digital tools, no account minimums, and simplified pricing. Most sizeable advisory firms end up integrating with more than one custodian rather than just one.

In practiceBecause almost every wealth-management data program eventually has to build a custodian integration, this is one of the highest-value concepts to understand cold.
Charles SchwabCustodian

Charles Schwab & Co., through its Schwab Advisor Services division, is one of the largest and most established custodians serving independent Registered Investment Advisors in the U.S. It provides custody, trade execution, and account administration for client assets, along with the technology and support services advisors rely on to run their practices — account opening, reporting integrations, and consultative support for growing a firm.

As a registered broker-dealer and SIPC member, Schwab is subject to extensive regulatory oversight designed to protect assets held in accounts at the firm. Its scale is a major differentiator: Schwab custodies several trillion dollars in advisory assets across well over ten thousand advisory firms, more than any other custodian, which is part of why so many wealth-management data programs treat a Schwab integration as a default rather than an edge case.

In practiceA named custodian integration like this is typically one of a small, explicitly-scoped set the business logic is built around — not an open-ended list.
Direct BusinessConcept

Assets or accounts held directly by the firm itself, rather than through an outside custodian relationship. Direct business typically needs its own data-handling rules, since it doesn't arrive through a standardized custodian feed the way custodied accounts do.

In practiceDirect business is often the trickier of any two paired integrations, precisely because there's no external custodian format to lean on.
Process & Delivery
Knowledge Transfer (KT) SessionProcess

A working session, usually led by whoever already knows a system or domain, designed to transfer that knowledge to a new or growing team — walking through file structures, logic, and history rather than just handing over documentation.

In practiceEffective KT is often two-track: one thread onboarding people to the technical environment, another onboarding them to the business/domain context — both matter equally.
Phased RolloutProcess

Delivering a program in stages over time, rather than launching everything at once (a "big bang" release). Each phase typically expands scope, user population, or functionality once the prior phase has proven stable.

In practiceAn initial client-facing release usually lands well before the program is fully "done" — the remaining phases continue to expand coverage afterward.
Data PracticeConcept

A formalized, repeatable organizational capability for managing data — standards, roles, tooling, and process — as opposed to data work happening ad hoc, project by project. Building one is a long-term investment that outlasts any single implementation.

In practiceA single high-visibility implementation like a client-portal build is often used as the proof point that justifies investing in a broader, lasting data practice.
